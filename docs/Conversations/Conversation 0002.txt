Absolutely. Here’s a “Venn-ready” mapping you can later turn into a diagram, but it’s written as a structured comparison: what Democrats say (platform), what Republicans say (platform), where they overlap, where they conflict, and a separate “platform ↔ practice tension” section for each.

I’m anchoring “written policy” to the 2024 national party platforms (DNC + RNC), since those are the clearest, official statements of intent.

⸻

Part 2 Framework

How to read this

For each topic:

1. Overlap (potential coalition ground / “intersections”)
2. Democratic emphasis (platform-anchored)
3. Republican emphasis (platform-anchored)
4. Conflict line (where narratives and policy diverge)
5. Platform ↔ practice tensions (where behavior often clashes with stated principles)

⸻

A. Purpose of Government

Overlap

* Both parties argue government should protect people from harm and keep the nation functioning (security, stability, infrastructure, etc.).

Democrats emphasize

* Government as an active instrument to expand opportunity, reduce inequality, and provide/coordinate broad social programs.

Republicans emphasize

* Government as protector of order, sovereignty, and “common sense,” with a strong emphasis on border enforcement, energy, and public safety.

Conflict line

* “Government as provider/manager” vs “government as protector/enforcer.”

Platform ↔ practice tensions (examples)

* Both parties routinely sustain large deficits and rely on continuing resolutions/omnibus budgeting—behavior that contradicts “disciplined, accountable governance” messaging in different ways.

⸻

B. Law and Order and the Role of Police

Overlap

* Both now publicly reject political violence and generally support public safety as a core function.
* Both have moved away from the broadest “abolish/defund” rhetoric (at least at the national platform level) and toward a “safety + reform” posture.

Democrats emphasize

* Policing plus reforms: accountability, civil rights enforcement, community programs, and gun safety as part of violence reduction.

Republicans emphasize

* Stronger policing and punishment posture: “back the blue,” crackdown on crime, and public order framing (often tied to border and drug policy).

Conflict line

* Accountability/reform-first framing vs enforcement/punishment-first framing
* Guns: Democrats often treat guns as a driver of crime; Republicans treat guns as a deterrent and a right.

Platform ↔ practice tensions

* Democrats: internal tension between national messaging (fund/reform) and activist/progressive local policies in some jurisdictions (this becomes a credibility gap in campaigns).
* Republicans: “law and order” rhetoric can collide with civil-liberties concerns when expanding surveillance, aggressive policing tactics, or qualified-immunity-like debates at state/federal levels (varies widely by faction).

⸻

C. Civil Liberties: Speech, Privacy, Due Process

Overlap

* Both claim to defend constitutional rights.
* Both frequently carve out exceptions under “safety,” “extremism,” “national security,” or “misinformation.”

Democrats emphasize

* Civil rights / anti-discrimination enforcement, hate-crime posture, and guarding democracy institutions.

Republicans emphasize

* Speech and viewpoint concerns (“censorship,” institutional bias), gun rights, and resistance to administrative overreach.

Conflict line

* Democrats tend to prioritize harm reduction / equity enforcement even if it expands administrative power.
* Republicans tend to prioritize constraint on institutions even if it reduces regulatory enforcement.

Platform ↔ practice tensions (high-value “Libertarian wedge”)

* Surveillance is the classic bipartisan contradiction: broad national-security surveillance authorities repeatedly pass with bipartisan support, despite civil-liberties objections. Section 702 reauthorization/expansion criticism is a concrete example.

⸻

D. Regulation and “Corporate Power”

Overlap

* Both say they oppose “rigged systems,” special interests, and unfairness.
* Both end up supporting policies that advantage favored industries.

Democrats emphasize

* Regulation for consumer protection, labor, environment, and anti-discrimination—government as a counterweight to corporate power.

Republicans emphasize

* Deregulation, energy expansion, and lowering barriers—markets and production as national strength.

Conflict line

* Regulation as protection vs regulation as barrier/cartel-builder

Platform ↔ practice tensions

* Both sides are vulnerable to the charge that regulation is used to pick winners (just with different preferred winners). This is exactly where a realist Libertarian position can say: “neutral rules, no preferential treatment.”

⸻

E. Taxes, Spending, and the “Size of the State”

Overlap

* Both support targeted tax preferences and carve-outs for constituencies (even while condemning “loopholes” rhetorically).
* Both tolerate or enable structural deficits when politically expedient.

Democrats emphasize

* Redistribution via tax code and spending; more robust federal role.

Republicans emphasize

* Tax reduction; growth and production; often paired with border/security and defense.

Conflict line

* Progressive tax/spend vs tax-cut / growth framing (with both often avoiding the hard deficit math)

Platform ↔ practice tensions (useful factual anchor)

* Independent deficit tracking shows persistent trillion-scale deficits and large federal spending realities that neither party fully resolves.

⸻

F. Federal vs State Power

Overlap

* Both appeal to “local control” when it helps them and to federal action when it doesn’t.
* Both selectively prefer “laboratories of democracy” only for issues they can tolerate losing on.

Democrats emphasize

* Federal standards/rights guarantees (civil rights, healthcare access, etc.).

Republicans emphasize

* State primacy (especially on social issues and education), federalism as restraint.

Conflict line

* National uniformity vs state variation.

⸻

G. Immigration and Borders (since it touches law/order)

Overlap

* Both increasingly acknowledge border capacity and enforcement matters (even if solutions differ).
* Both talk about “fixing the system,” yet long-term legislative resolution often stalls.

Democrats emphasize

* Humane enforcement + pathways/legal process framing.

Republicans emphasize

* Border closure/pressure and deterrence-first approach.

Conflict line

* Humanitarian framing vs sovereignty/deterrence framing.

⸻

H. Foreign Policy and Military Posture

Overlap

* Both support a strong defense (at least rhetorically).
* Both authorize expansive executive power in national security over time.

Democrats emphasize

* Alliances and international leadership framing.

Republicans emphasize

* “America First” posture and burden-sharing framing.

Conflict line

* Internationalist leadership vs nationalist restraint.

⸻

The “Libertarian Intersections” this mapping reveals (high leverage)

These are places Libertarians can win votes from both sides without surrendering principles:

1. Anti-crony, pro-competition regulation (“no rules that cartelize industries”)
2. Civil liberties + surveillance restraint (the bipartisan weak spot)
3. Budget transparency and auditable spending (pair with deficit reality)
4. Federalism with a rule: “default local unless enumerated / constitutionally required”
5. Police = necessary, but bounded: support law enforcement + strict accountability + due process
