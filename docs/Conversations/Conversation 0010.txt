# PART 2.6 — Sanctuary Policies, Federalism, and Consent

### Jurisdiction, Constitutional Boundaries, and Lawful Resistance

## I. The First Principle: Sovereignty Has a Direction

Libertarianism begins with a hierarchy that modern politics has inverted:

> **Sovereignty originates in the people.**

From there, authority is delegated *upward*:

1. Individuals
2. Local (municipal / county) governance
3. State governments
4. Federal government

Each layer exists only by **delegated authority**, and each layer is constrained to its proper lane.

This is not ideology.
This is the structural logic of the Constitution itself.

---

## II. Federalism Is Not Optional

The Constitution creates a **federal system**, not a unitary state.

* The federal government has **enumerated powers**
* All other powers are **reserved to the states or the people** (10th Amendment)
* States are sovereign within their sphere, but **not supreme**

This means:

* States are not “subordinate departments”
* Municipalities are creatures of the state
* Federal law applies **only where it is constitutional**

Federal supremacy exists—but only within constitutional bounds.

---

## III. Immigration Authority: What the Constitution Actually Says

Immigration law is primarily federal because:

* naturalization is enumerated to Congress
* foreign relations are federal
* border control implicates national defense
* interstate and foreign commerce are involved

So here is the Libertarian baseline, stated clearly:

> **The federal government has legitimate authority to set immigration policy and enforce immigration law—provided that law itself is constitutional.**

That is the starting point. Not the ending point.

---

## IV. The 10th Amendment: What States May and May Not Do

The 10th Amendment does **not** give states the power to:

* nullify federal law outright
* obstruct lawful federal enforcement
* create parallel immigration systems

But it **does** mean:

* the federal government cannot *commandeer* state resources
* states are not required to enforce federal law for the feds
* states retain police powers over health, safety, and welfare *within their jurisdiction*

This distinction matters enormously.

---

## V. The Anti-Commandeering Doctrine (The Legal Spine)

The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that:

> **The federal government may not compel states or local governments to enforce federal regulatory programs.**

This is not theory—it is settled doctrine.

That means:

* states cannot be forced to use state police to enforce federal immigration law
* local governments cannot be required to detain individuals solely on federal request
* cooperation must be **voluntary**, not coerced

This is the **constitutional basis sanctuary advocates rely on**—and they are not wrong *on this narrow point*.

But that is not the whole story.

---

## VI. Where Sanctuary Policies Go Off the Rails

Libertarian realism draws a sharp line between **non-cooperation** and **active obstruction**.

### Constitutionally defensible:

* declining to use state resources for federal enforcement
* requiring judicial warrants for detention
* prioritizing state law enforcement duties

### Constitutionally problematic:

* prohibiting communication with federal authorities
* shielding individuals from lawful federal process
* releasing detainees despite valid federal warrants
* creating policies designed to frustrate enforcement rather than remain neutral

When a state or city crosses from *non-participation* into *interference*, it is no longer exercising federalism—it is **undermining the rule of law**.

---

## VII. Municipal “Sanctuary” Policies Are Especially Problematic

Here’s a key point Libertarians must not dodge:

> **Cities do not possess sovereignty.**

Municipalities:

* exist at the pleasure of the state
* have no independent constitutional authority
* cannot override state or federal law

A city declaring itself a “sanctuary” against federal immigration law is:

* not exercising constitutional power
* asserting authority it does not possess
* potentially violating state law

From a libertarian perspective, this is **illegitimate decentralization**—power moving sideways without consent.

---

## VIII. The 14th Amendment: Equal Protection and Due Process

The 14th Amendment complicates this conversation—but does not erase federal authority.

It guarantees:

* due process to all persons
* equal protection under law

This means:

* immigrants cannot be punished without process
* enforcement must be individualized
* states cannot selectively deny basic protections

But it does **not** mean:

* states may override immigration law
* unlawful presence becomes a protected status
* local governments may nullify federal authority

Due process constrains *how* law is enforced—not *whether* it may be enforced.

---

## IX. Commerce Clause and General Welfare: The Danger Zones

You’re right to flag these.

### Commerce Clause

Immigration affects:

* labor markets
* interstate commerce
* foreign trade

Courts have used this to justify broad federal authority.

Libertarian caution:

* commerce power must not become a backdoor police power
* not every human movement is “commerce”
* limits still matter

### General Welfare Clause

This clause authorizes taxation and spending—not unlimited regulation.

It cannot:

* override enumerated powers
* erase federalism
* justify permanent emergency governance

Libertarian realism insists:

> These clauses justify *means*, not *ends without limit*.

---

## X. So… Are Sanctuary Cities Legal?

Here is the honest, non-slogan answer:

* **Non-cooperation with federal enforcement** → generally constitutional
* **Active obstruction or shielding** → likely unconstitutional
* **Municipal sanctuary declarations** → legally weakest and most problematic

A Libertarian position must say this plainly:

> **Federal law must be enforced lawfully.
> States may decline to assist—but may not sabotage.
> Cities have no authority to nullify.**

---

## XI. Lawful Pushback: How States Resist Without Insurrection

Libertarianism rejects both:

* blind compliance with unconstitutional law
* performative resistance that breaks the system

Instead, lawful resistance includes:

1. **Litigation**

   * challenge unconstitutional statutes or practices in court

2. **Refusal to fund or administer**

   * decline participation without obstruction

3. **Clear statutory limits**

   * states defining what their agencies will and won’t do

4. **Transparency**

   * open reporting of enforcement interactions

5. **Political accountability**

   * elections, legislation, amendments

This is how free societies resolve jurisdictional conflict—without violence or collapse.

---

## XII. Consent Is the Thread That Holds This Together

Sanctuary policy fails when it:

* ignores the consent of residents
* shifts costs to unwilling communities
* overrides property and safety concerns
* operates without democratic accountability

Liberty does not mean **someone else absorbs the consequences of your policy choices**.

Consent must be:

* local
* explicit
* reversible

---

## Manifesto-Ready Summary

Immigration law is a legitimate federal function when exercised constitutionally.
The 10th Amendment forbids federal commandeering but does not authorize obstruction.
States may decline participation but must not interfere with lawful enforcement.
Municipal sanctuary policies lack sovereign authority and are constitutionally weak.
The 14th Amendment guarantees due process—not nullification.
Libertarianism demands lawful enforcement, lawful resistance, and consent at every level.
