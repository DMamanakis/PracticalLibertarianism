PART 2.3 — Property Rights, Public Land, and Necessary Commons

A Libertarian Realist Framework for Roads, Borders, and Infrastructure

I. The Principle We Do Not Surrender

Libertarianism begins with a non-negotiable moral truth:

Individuals have a right to acquire, hold, use, and exclude others from property.

Property rights are not a luxury. They are the mechanism of consent in a free society:

* what you may do
* what you may refuse
* what you may trade
* what you may defend

Without property rights, “rights” become slogans that depend on whoever controls the state.

So the Libertarian default is:

* private ownership
* voluntary exchange
* enforceable exclusion
* neutral courts and rules

That stays.

⸻

II. The Realist Problem: Some Things Function Like “Network Property”

Libertarianism also has to deal with a practical reality:

Some systems are not like farms and houses. They’re networks:

* roads
* bridges
* ports of entry
* navigation corridors
* border crossings
* water rights infrastructure (in some regions)
* emergency corridors

These have two characteristics:

1. They require continuity.
    A road that stops being a road breaks the network.
2. They require common standards.
    A transportation network without shared rules becomes dangerous and economically brittle.

If every “link” in a road network could be closed by an heir, a creditor, or a grudge:

* commerce becomes hostage
* travel becomes hostage
* the economy becomes fragile
* rights become subject to private choke points

So the question is not “private vs government.”
It’s:

How do we preserve property rights while guaranteeing continuity of essential networks?

⸻

III. The Libertarian Realist Answer: Keep Ownership Private, Make Access Contractual

A serious Libertarian model does not require the state to “own everything,” but it also does not pretend networks run themselves.

A workable bridge principle is:

Networks can be privately owned and publicly accessible through contracts, easements, and user-fee systems—without converting them into permanent government property.

This is not “statism.”
It’s simply ownership + contract at scale.

Mechanisms that fit Libertarian ethics

* Co-ops (member-owned infrastructure)
* Road corporations / trusts (profit or non-profit)
* Easements (legal right-of-way that runs with the land)
* Long-term concessions (performance-bound and revocable)
* Tolls / per-mile fees (paid by users, not non-users)
* Axle-weight pricing (damage proportionality)
* Insurance-linked access (no insurance, no access)

None of these requires a permanent government monopoly.

⸻

IV. Roads: Your “Old Man Jones” Problem Is Real—and Solvable

Your example is the exact failure mode for “pure” privatization:

* A critical link becomes a hostage to inheritance, neglect, or indifference.
* The network fractures.
* Everyone’s liberty shrinks.

Libertarian realism solves it by separating:

* ownership
* from guaranteed access

The Road Co-op Model (Manifesto-ready)

1. Roads are owned by a state-level co-op (or multi-state compact co-op).
2. Membership/usage requires:
    * proof of insurance
    * agreement to rules of the road
    * payment of user fees (toll / mileage / weight class)
3. The co-op enforces continuity via:
    * maintenance obligations
    * professional management
    * bonding requirements
    * transparent audits
4. Local enforcement is handled by:
    * contracted local police or
    * a limited “road marshal” service (civil enforcement + safety), strictly constrained by due process

This keeps:

* property rights intact
* access reliable
* rules uniform
* costs borne by users
* and it avoids making “the state” the permanent landlord of transportation

Why this is Libertarian (the honest reason)

Because it is:

* voluntary (opt-in by use)
* fee-based (not coercive general taxation)
* accountable (audits + contracts + courts)
* decentralized (state-by-state with compacts)
* rights-respecting (rules are contractual, not arbitrary decrees)

⸻

V. Borders and Ports of Entry Need “Reliable Network Property”

Now tie it back to the border, cleanly:

In a property-rights society, a border is not a moral symbol—it’s an exclusion boundary. Entry happens through consent.

Ports of entry require:

* reliable road corridors
* inspection facilities
* traffic management
* quarantine capacity (when needed)
* secure holding space for adjudication (when needed)
* clear jurisdiction

This is not “big government.”
It’s the minimum physical infrastructure required to make lawful entry possible.

If lawful entry is cumbersome, people evade it.
So a libertarian society must make lawful entry easy—not because borders don’t matter, but because law must be usable.

⸻

VI. The Minimum Entry Conditions You Described (Refined)

A rights-respecting system can require, at entry:

1. Identity verification
2. Criminal background screen (within reason and due process)
3. Health attestation / screening
    * The goal is not medical tyranny
    * The goal is preventing predictable, large-scale public harm
4. Proof of self-support or verified employment / sponsor
5. Clear terms of stay and a trackable legal status

And here’s the key libertarian framing:

These are not moral judgments. They are conditions of entry onto a rights-based territory, the same way a property owner may set conditions for entry onto his land.

⸻

VII. “They Should Bring Money” Is Not Cruel—It’s Anti-Exploitation

This is worth stating plainly in your manifesto:

A society that allows mass entry without:

* legal work authorization
* housing arrangements
* financial means
* enforceable labor protections

…creates an illegal underclass.

That underclass is:

* abused by employers
* abused by criminals
* politicized by activists
* and used as a pressure valve on wages and housing

So requiring money, sponsors, or verified work is not “anti-immigrant.”
It’s pro-human dignity.

⸻

VIII. Enforcement: Who Polices Roads and Borders in a Minarchy?

Here’s the principle that keeps you from sounding “anti-police” or authoritarian:

Enforcement exists to protect rights and uphold contracts, not to engineer outcomes or punish disfavored groups.

Borders

* National defense and border integrity are legitimate federal functions.
* But enforcement must be:
    * rule-bound
    * auditable
    * proportional
    * accountable

Roads

* Safety enforcement is legitimate because reckless driving violates others’ rights.
* But enforcement should be:
    * civil-first where possible
    * escalatory only when necessary
    * tied to specific harm/risk, not revenue extraction

A co-op can contract enforcement the same way a stadium does:

* you can be removed for violations
* you can be civilly fined
* you can be criminally charged if you commit real crimes

⸻

IX. Government Ownership: The Realist Concession (and the constraint)

You said it well: centralism isn’t always bad.

A manifesto-friendly way to state this without losing libertarian principle:

Libertarianism prefers private ownership, but recognizes that where continuity, national defense, and network integrity are indispensable, narrowly tailored public ownership or stewardship can be tolerated—provided it is bounded, audited, non-expansionary, and designed to be privatizable over time.

That gives you realism without ideological surrender.

Examples that can plausibly fit:

* limited border facilities
* ports of entry
* essential corridors where easements are impractical

But with strict constraints:

* no mission creep
* no unrelated land grabs
* no permanent bureaucracy growth
* clear performance metrics and sunset review
