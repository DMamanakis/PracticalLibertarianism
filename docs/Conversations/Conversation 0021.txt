## 1. Mail-in Voting

You asked:

> Is it true that many countries have abandoned mail-in voting over fraud concerns?

The answer is **partially, but not in the broad way it's often stated.**

Many democracies **do not** use widespread no-excuse universal mail voting like several U.S. states. Instead they emphasize in-person voting, national voter registries, and voter identification. Some allow absentee voting only for specific reasons, while others permit postal voting more broadly. Separately, a number of countries have abandoned or limited **electronic/Internet voting** because of security or transparency concerns, which is a different issue from paper ballots sent through the mail. ([OECD][1])

So I wouldn't build your argument around:

> "Most countries banned mail voting because of fraud."

That's too broad and is easy to rebut.

A stronger argument is:

> Mail voting creates additional chain-of-custody and verification challenges that require correspondingly stronger safeguards if public confidence is to be maintained.

That's hard to dispute.

## 2. The 2020-Type Claims

You listed several things:

* ballot spikes
* late counting
* ballots discovered later
* more votes than voters
* duplicate registrations
* non-citizens on rolls
* counting after deadlines
* etc.

These are different kinds of claims, and they need to be treated separately.

Some are **documented administrative issues** that have occurred in various jurisdictions—for example:

* duplicate voter registrations,
* outdated voter rolls,
* chain-of-custody concerns,
* counting delays,
* ballots arriving after Election Day where state law permitted it.

Some claims have been investigated repeatedly without evidence that they changed election outcomes or reflected widespread fraud, such as allegations of large-scale coordinated fraud in the 2020 presidential election. ([Reuters][2])

For your book, I'd avoid relying on claims that remain disputed or unsupported.

Instead, make the structural argument:

> Even isolated administrative failures, inconsistent procedures, or prolonged uncertainty can undermine public confidence in elections.

That point stands regardless of whether fraud is ultimately proven.

# 3. Confidence Matters

This is where I think your manifesto can add something valuable.

An election has **two objectives**:

* produce the correct winner;
* convince the public that the winner is correct.

Those are different.

If 40–50% of the country believes something improper happened—even if it didn't—that is itself a problem for a republic.

Government therefore has an interest in designing systems that are:

* secure;
* transparent;
* auditable;
* understandable.

# 4. Hard Rules

Here I think you're onto something.

Instead of leaving every state to invent its own rules, I think a constitutional libertarian model could support **minimum federal standards for federal elections**.

Not because Washington knows best.

Because Congress has an express constitutional role in regulating the "Times, Places and Manner" of congressional elections.

Those standards could include things like:

### Registration

* citizenship verified
* residency verified
* duplicate registration checked
* free correction process

### Ballots

* unique serial tracking (without linking to voter identity)
* documented chain of custody
* bipartisan handling
* secure storage

### Counting

* counting begins when polls close (or earlier processing if state law allows, provided no results are released before polls close)
* continuous counting until completion except for documented emergencies
* every interruption publicly documented
* live public observation where practical

### Deadlines

* ballots must satisfy uniform statutory deadlines established before the election
* deadlines may not be altered after voting begins except by lawful judicial order addressing a constitutional issue

### Audits

* mandatory risk-limiting audits
* random precinct audits
* automatic recount thresholds
* preservation of all election materials

None of these favor one party.

They simply increase confidence.

# 6. California Problem

You asked:

> What if California simply refuses to enforce election integrity?

That's the difficult constitutional question.

I don't think the answer is:

> Washington takes over California elections.

That destroys federalism.

Instead:

If a state fails to meet **minimum constitutional standards** for federal elections, Congress could authorize narrowly tailored remedies tied to those federal contests. Those remedies should be specific, temporary, and reviewable by the courts—not an open-ended federal takeover.

That preserves both accountability and the constitutional balance.

Knowingly casting an illegal ballot.

Altering counts.

Coercing voters.

Conspiring to manipulate results.

Lifetime disqualification from election administration or public office.
