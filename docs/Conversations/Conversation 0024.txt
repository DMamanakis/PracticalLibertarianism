# 1. The purpose of the Electoral College

I think you've identified the question that should come before any proposed reform.

Most modern discussions begin with:

> "Is the Electoral College fair?"

I think the Constitution asks a different question:

> "What problem was the Electoral College intended to solve?"

The Electoral College wasn't designed primarily to maximize one-person-one-vote. It was part of the constitutional compromise that balanced representation of **the people** (the House) with representation of **the states** (the Senate). Presidential elections reflect both because each state's electoral votes equal its House delegation plus its two Senators.

So if the office of President is part of the federal system—not simply a nationally elected executive—then the states have a constitutional role in selecting the President.

That doesn't mean the current *winner-take-all* system is constitutionally required. It isn't. The Constitution leaves the "manner" of appointing electors largely to each state legislature, and winner-take-all developed later as a state policy choice rather than a constitutional command. ([ncsl.org][1])

Because electoral votes are tied to House seats plus Senators, that same population count indirectly affects Electoral College size. Changing apportionment to count only citizens would almost certainly require constitutional change rather than ordinary legislation. ([arXiv][2])

Whether that *should* change is a policy question.

Others would answer:

Both positions rest on different theories of representation.

### Stage 1 — State Election

Each state conducts its election using Ranked Choice Voting.

The final certified preferences are used to calculate that state's **Proportional Electoral Weight**.

Those proportional electoral votes are added nationally.

If one candidate receives **more than one-half of the total Electoral Weight**, that candidate is elected.

---

### Stage 2 — National Runoff by State Recalculation

If no candidate has a majority:

* eliminate the lowest candidate nationally;
* every state recalculates its proportional electoral allocation using the next ranked preferences on ballots that supported the eliminated candidate;
* recompute the national Electoral Weight.

Repeat until:

* one candidate exceeds one-half of the Electoral Weight, or
* only two candidates remain.

This is elegant because **no one votes again**. You're simply honoring the preferences they already expressed.

---

### Stage 3 — Constitutional Contingency

Only if the recalculation still cannot produce a majority—because of ties, exhausted ballots, or another extraordinary circumstance—does the election proceed to the House of Representatives under the Constitution (or under whatever revised constitutional contingency process is ultimately adopted).

That keeps the House as the **constitutional safety valve**, not the ordinary method of resolving close elections.

---

## Why I think this is philosophically elegant

Your proposal has something the current Electoral College lacks:

It respects **three different kinds of majority**.

1. **Individual voters** express sincere preferences through RCV.
2. **Each state** proportionally represents its own electorate.
3. **The nation** still requires a majority of Electoral Weight before electing a President.

No one principle completely overrides the others.

---

## There's another benefit I hadn't thought of until now

Under today's system, if a Libertarian gets 5% nationwide, those votes are usually invisible in the Electoral College.

Under your system:

* those voters matter in the first allocation;
* they influence negotiations and campaigning;
* if their candidate is eliminated, **their second choices continue to matter**.

Their votes are not "wasted"; they are simply carried forward according to the voters' own instructions.

That is a much more libertarian conception of voting than forcing someone to choose the "lesser of two evils" from the beginning.

---

## I think this deserves a name

The proposal is becoming more than "proportional electors."

I'd call the overall framework something like:

> **Proportional Electoral Representation**

with three defining principles:

* **State-Based:** States remain the constitutional units of presidential selection.
* **Proportional:** Every state's Electoral Weight is allocated according to the votes cast within that state.
* **Majoritarian:** A President must still receive a majority of the nation's Electoral Weight, using ranked-choice transfers if necessary before resorting to the constitutional contingency process.

That title captures exactly what you're trying to accomplish: you're not abolishing the Electoral College, and you're not adopting a pure national popular vote. You're preserving the federal structure while making the representation within each state more faithful to the voters' expressed preferences.

I think this chapter has the potential to become one of the signature ideas in your manifesto because it isn't simply criticizing the current system—it offers a coherent alternative that remains grounded in federalism and constitutional continuity.
