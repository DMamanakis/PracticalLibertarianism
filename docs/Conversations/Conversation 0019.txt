Yes. That is probably the best way to reconcile **state sovereignty** with **national military effectiveness**.

The model would be:

> **State command, common doctrine, interoperable equipment, and temporary federal integration when constitutionally activated.**

Your NIMS/ICS analogy is very good, with one important refinement: when federal personnel arrive under ICS, they do not automatically “take over.” Depending on jurisdiction and legal authority, they may join a Unified Command, support the existing command, or receive command through a formally documented transfer. NIMS deliberately combines standardization with flexibility and unity of effort. ([FEMA][1])

That is exactly the distinction our military model needs:

* **Interoperability does not require centralized ownership.**
* **Standardization does not require permanent federal command.**
* **Coordination does not require the states to surrender their forces.**

There is one constitutional nuance. Article I presently gives Congress power to provide for “organizing, arming, and disciplining” the militia and reserves to the states the appointment of officers and training according to the discipline prescribed by Congress. Thus, under the existing Constitution, Congress may establish more than merely informal suggestions. ([Congress.gov][2])

For your reformed system, I would divide federal standards into two categories:

### Baseline constitutional standards

Congress could establish the minimum conditions necessary for forces that may enter federal service:

* rank equivalencies;
* command terminology;
* communications protocols;
* medical standards;
* identification systems;
* ammunition and equipment compatibility;
* training certifications;
* readiness reporting;
* laws of armed conflict;
* rules governing federal activation.

These standards would be binding only where genuine interoperability is necessary.

### Voluntary operational doctrine

The federal strategic command could publish broader recommended doctrine covering:

* unit organization;
* training exercises;
* logistics procedures;
* maintenance practices;
* mobilization planning;
* specialty qualifications;
* regional mutual-aid procedures.

States could adopt these through legislation, interstate compacts, or defense agreements. Federal equipment and readiness funding could be conditioned on compliance with the relevant interoperability standards, but Washington could not use the funding mechanism to take permanent control of the state forces.

Here is how I would revise the chapter.

## Federal Interoperability Without Permanent Federal Control

A distributed military does not require the states to choose between sovereignty and effectiveness.

The country already uses a comparable model in emergency management. Under the National Incident Management System and Incident Command System, local, state, federal, private, and volunteer organizations train in a shared structure. They use common terminology, recognizable positions, compatible procedures, resource classifications, communications plans, and command principles.

When an emergency expands, additional personnel do not need to invent a new organization. They enter positions that already exist within the structure. Units from different jurisdictions can work together because each understands the same operational language.

National defense should employ the same general principle:

> State forces should remain independent in ownership and ordinary command while using nationally compatible doctrine, training, equipment, and command structures.

### State Authority

In ordinary circumstances, each state defense force would remain under the authority of:

* the governor;
* state-appointed officers;
* the state legislature;
* state military law;
* state funding and oversight.

The state would determine:

* the size of its force;
* its internal organization;
* the selection and promotion of officers;
* its particular specialties;
* its ordinary missions;
* the conditions under which it may assist another state.

No federal department would exercise routine command merely because a state force followed national standards or used federally compatible equipment.

### Common Defense Standards

Congress would establish only those minimum standards reasonably necessary to permit state forces to operate together and to enter federal service when constitutionally called.

Those standards could include:

* common rank classifications;
* compatible command structures;
* standardized map and operational symbols;
* secure communications protocols;
* equipment interfaces;
* ammunition compatibility;
* medical and casualty procedures;
* logistics terminology;
* personnel qualification standards;
* unit-readiness classifications;
* laws governing military conduct;
* procedures for federal activation and release.

These standards would create a common military language. They would not determine every detail of state organization.

A state could exceed the federal standard, adopt different equipment where compatibility was preserved, or organize its forces differently where those differences did not prevent effective joint operations.

### Federal Guidelines and State Adoption

Beyond minimum interoperability requirements, the federal strategic defense establishment could issue recommended doctrine and model procedures.

These would function as guidelines rather than ordinary federal commands.

States could adopt them:

* individually through state law;
* collectively through interstate compact;
* contractually when accepting particular federal equipment;
* voluntarily when participating in regional training or federal readiness programs.

The purpose would be to help every state prepare for the same general operating environment without converting state forces into subsidiaries of the Department of Defense.

### The Plug-and-Play Principle

Every qualified unit should have an established place in the larger defense structure before an emergency begins.

A state aviation wing should know:

* which regional command it supports;
* what air-defense sector it enters;
* which communications frequencies and encryption systems it uses;
* which bases can supply and repair its aircraft;
* who receives its operational reports;
* how command changes if it enters federal service.

An engineering battalion should already know:

* its equipment classification;
* its transportation requirements;
* the bridges, airfields, ports, or utilities it may be assigned to restore;
* how it requests materials;
* how it integrates with neighboring-state and federal engineering units.

Medical, cyber, logistics, coastal, infantry, and transportation forces should be organized in the same fashion.

Mobilization should not begin with the question, “Where can we put these people?”

Their potential roles, reporting structures, equipment requirements, and mobilization locations should already be known.

### Mutual Assistance Between States

Before federal activation becomes necessary, states should be able to assist each other through preapproved interstate defense compacts.

A governor facing an attack or major emergency could request specified assistance from another governor, including:

* air-defense units;
* engineering teams;
* medical personnel;
* transportation assets;
* cyber specialists;
* logistics units;
* coastal or border forces.

The assisting units would remain under their home state’s legal authority unless the compact provided for temporary operational control by the receiving state.

The distinction between **command** and **operational control** must remain clear.

A receiving commander might direct the unit’s mission while the home state retains authority over:

* personnel administration;
* discipline;
* promotion;
* permanent assignment;
* removal of the unit from the mission.

This allows states to cooperate without surrendering ownership of their forces.

### Federal Participation and Unified Command

Federal arrival should not automatically extinguish state authority.

When the federal strategic force assists with an incident that remains principally under state jurisdiction, federal officers should enter a unified or coordinated command with the affected states.

The command structure would recognize:

* the governor’s authority over state forces;
* federal authority over federal forces;
* any statutory federal responsibility;
* the need for one coordinated operational plan.

Where jurisdictions overlap, a Unified Defense Command could establish common objectives while preserving each participant’s lawful authority.

For example, during an attack on a major coastal port:

* the state could command its Guard, police, emergency, and infrastructure forces;
* the federal Navy could command naval combat operations;
* the Coast Guard could exercise its lawful maritime functions;
* neighboring states could provide forces under interstate compact;
* all could operate from a single coordinated action plan.

No participant would need to surrender its separate sovereignty merely to cooperate.

### Transfer to Federal Command

A state unit would enter exclusive federal command only when lawfully called into the actual service of the United States.

The activation order should specify:

* the constitutional and statutory authority;
* the units activated;
* the mission;
* the geographic scope;
* the effective time;
* the duration;
* the federal chain of command;
* the conditions for return to state control.

Once activated, the unit would fit immediately into the national command structure because it had already trained under compatible doctrine.

When the activation ended, it would return fully to state command.

Federalization would therefore be a defined temporary legal condition—not the permanent identity of the force.

### Qualification Rather Than Ownership

The federal government should certify capability, not own every unit that provides it.

A state fighter wing could be certified as capable of national air-defense service.

A regional cyber unit could be certified to defend particular classes of critical systems.

A state medical formation could be certified to operate within a national casualty network.

Certification would establish that the unit meets the required standard. It would not alter the unit’s ordinary chain of command.

This resembles professional licensing or equipment certification more than federal absorption.

### Exercises and Readiness

Interoperability must be demonstrated, not assumed.

State, regional, and federal forces should conduct regular joint exercises involving:

* mobilization;
* communications;
* command transfer;
* unified command;
* air and missile defense;
* logistics;
* cyber response;
* casualty evacuation;
* infrastructure repair;
* coastal and border defense.

Exercises should test whether units can actually connect, communicate, receive missions, exchange supplies, and operate safely.

A state force that repeatedly fails readiness standards could lose federal certification or eligibility for federal equipment until it corrected the deficiency. Its existence as a state force, however, would not depend upon federal permission.

### National Standards as a Floor, Not a Federal Chain

The purpose of national standards is to make cooperation possible.

It is not to allow federal officials to dictate every state military decision.

The governing principle should be:

> The federal government defines the interface; the states design and operate the components.

This is the same principle that permits independently governed organizations to work within a common incident-management structure.

The states retain their forces.

The nation gains interoperability.

When assistance is needed, units can join one another without confusion.

When federal activation is constitutionally justified, the units can enter federal service without requiring months of reorganization.

When the mission ends, they return to the states from which their authority ordinarily derives.

I would also replace the chapter’s earlier either/or conclusion with this:

> The choice is not between a federally controlled National Guard and fifty incompatible state armies. A third model is available: genuinely state-controlled forces operating under common national interoperability standards. The states would own, officer, train, and ordinarily command their forces. Congress would define the minimum interface necessary for common defense, while broader doctrine would be adopted through state consent, interstate compact, and voluntary participation in federal readiness programs.

One wording change is especially important: rather than saying federal forces would “take over,” the manifesto should say they **integrate into the established command, form a Unified Defense Command, or assume command through a lawful transfer of authority**. That protects the very principle we are trying to preserve.
