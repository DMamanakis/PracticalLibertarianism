# Hostile Review Process

## Purpose

A “Hostile Review” is not an exercise in political hostility, personal
hostility, or indiscriminate criticism.

The purpose is not to destroy the document, ridicule its conclusions, or
manufacture objections merely for the sake of disagreement.

The purpose is to strengthen the document and test whether it is capable of
accomplishing the task for which it was written.

The manuscript should be able to withstand serious scrutiny from readers holding
different political philosophies, constitutional interpretations, economic
assumptions, moral beliefs, and views of government.

Within this project, *hostile* means:

> **Adversarial, rigorous, skeptical, and critically challenging.**

The reviewer should approach the document with the working assumption:

> “Assume the author may be wrong. Attempt to identify where, why, and how.”

This is different from acting in bad faith.

A useful Hostile Review challenges the document while remaining accurate, fair,
and evidence-based.

## Place in the Project Workflow

The standard project workflow is:

1. Research
2. Outline
3. Draft
4. Constitutional Review
5. **Hostile Review**
6. Technical and Factual Review
7. Citation Review
8. Editorial Review
9. Publish

The Hostile Review occurs after the argument has been researched, structured,
drafted, and reviewed for constitutional consistency.

It occurs before the final technical, factual, citation, and editorial reviews
because it may expose weaknesses that require additional research, revision, or
restructuring.

## Review Objective

As the manuscript approaches its final phases, each major chapter or argument
should be subjected to deliberate challenge.

The Hostile Review should determine whether:

- The argument is logically sound.
- The factual foundation is sufficient.
- Important assumptions are disclosed.
- Definitions are precise and consistently applied.
- Relevant counterarguments have been considered.
- Significant opposing evidence has been addressed.
- Constitutional claims are adequately supported.
- Proposed solutions follow from the identified problems.
- The document distinguishes present reality from preferred reform.
- The argument remains understandable to readers who do not share the author’s
  political philosophy.

The goal is not to anticipate every possible objection.

The goal is to ensure that foreseeable, material, and credible objections have
been considered before publication.

## Core Review Questions

The reviewer should attempt to answer the following questions.

### Logical Review

- Are there logical inconsistencies?
- Do any conclusions fail to follow from the stated premises?
- Are there contradictions within the chapter or between related chapters?
- Does the argument rely on circular reasoning?
- Are correlations being treated as causation?
- Are exceptions being used as though they establish a general rule?
- Does the argument create a false choice between limited alternatives?
- Are any material steps in the reasoning missing?

### Assumption Review

- What assumptions are explicit?
- What assumptions are hidden?
- Which assumptions are unsupported?
- Which assumptions depend upon a particular political, moral, constitutional,
  or economic worldview?
- Would the argument still hold if one or more assumptions were false?
- Does the proposal depend upon people, institutions, or officials behaving more
  responsibly than current incentives suggest?

### Definition and Clarity Review

- Are important terms defined?
- Are definitions precise?
- Are terms used consistently?
- Could a reasonable reader interpret key language differently?
- Does the chapter distinguish fact, interpretation, opinion, and proposal?
- Does it distinguish current law from preferred reform?
- Are constitutional, statutory, regulatory, and policy concepts kept separate?

### Factual Review

- Are the factual claims accurate?
- Is relevant context missing?
- Are statistics presented fairly?
- Are dates, figures, quotations, and historical claims correct?
- Are there contrary facts or datasets that should be acknowledged?
- Is information current enough for the claim being made?
- Are claims broader than the evidence supports?

### Constitutional and Legal Review

- Is the relevant constitutional authority identified?
- Is constitutional text distinguished from judicial interpretation?
- Is current law distinguished from original public meaning?
- Are contrary judicial precedents or legal interpretations acknowledged?
- Does the proposal require statutory change, constitutional amendment,
  administrative reform, or judicial reconsideration?
- Does the proposal preserve due process, equal protection, federalism, and
  separation of powers?
- Does the argument claim that something is unconstitutional when it is more
  accurately described as undesirable, unwise, or inconsistent with the
  project’s preferred interpretation?

### Counterargument Review

- What is the strongest reasonable argument against the chapter’s conclusion?
- Has that argument been addressed fairly?
- Has the document attacked a weak version of the opposing position?
- Could an informed critic claim that a major objection was ignored?
- Are opposing motives being assumed without evidence?
- Are alternative explanations considered?
- Are there circumstances in which the opposing position would be reasonable?

### Proposal Review

- Does the proposal actually solve the identified problem?
- Is the proposal constitutionally authorized?
- Is it practical?
- Is it enforceable?
- What are the foreseeable unintended consequences?
- What incentives would it create?
- Could the proposed power be abused by future officials?
- Could the proposal produce the opposite of its intended result?
- What transition problems would arise?
- What would implementation cost?
- Who would bear those costs?
- What happens if the proposal only partially succeeds?

### Failure Review

The reviewer should directly answer:

> **How can this argument fail?**

This includes failure of:

- Facts
- Logic
- Definitions
- Constitutional interpretation
- Evidence
- Implementation
- Incentives
- Public understanding
- Political feasibility
- Institutional design
- Unintended consequences

## Multi-Agent Review Process

The same chapter or proposal may be submitted to multiple AI systems, including:

- ChatGPT
- Grok
- Claude
- Gemini

No AI system should be treated as an authority merely because it produced a
confident answer.

The purpose of using multiple systems is to increase the variety of challenges,
interpretations, and detected weaknesses.

Agreement among the systems may indicate that an issue deserves particular
attention, but agreement does not prove that the criticism is correct.

Likewise, a criticism raised by only one system should not be dismissed merely
because the others failed to identify it.

Every material criticism must be evaluated on its own merits.

## Review Methods

### Method 1 — Comprehensive Parallel Review

Give the same chapter to each AI system and instruct every system to evaluate all
review criteria.

The results are then compared.

#### Advantages

- Provides broad coverage.
- Reveals which issues are independently detected by multiple systems.
- Allows comparison of each system’s overall review quality.
- Reduces the risk that an assigned specialty prevents a reviewer from
  identifying an issue outside that specialty.

#### Disadvantages

- Reviews may be long and repetitive.
- Different systems may organize results differently.
- Important issues may be obscured by large volumes of minor criticism.

#### Recommended Use

This should be the default method for major chapters, major proposals, and final
prepublication review.

### Method 2 — Targeted Parallel Review

Give the same chapter to each AI system, but require all systems to examine one
specific criterion.

Examples:

- Logical consistency
- Constitutional analysis
- Factual accuracy
- Definition precision
- Counterarguments
- Implementation risk
- Citation quality

The results are then compared.

#### Advantages

- Produces focused and directly comparable reviews.
- Encourages deeper examination of a narrow issue.
- Makes it easier to identify differences in reasoning among systems.
- Works well when a known weakness requires special attention.

#### Disadvantages

- May miss problems outside the assigned criterion.
- Requires multiple review rounds to cover the entire document.
- Can create false confidence if a narrow review is mistaken for a complete
  review.

#### Recommended Use

Use this method after a comprehensive review, when a specific category requires
deeper analysis.

### Method 3 — Distributed Specialized Review

Divide the review criteria among the AI systems.

For example:

- One system reviews logic and assumptions.
- One reviews facts and missing context.
- One reviews constitutional and legal issues.
- One reviews clarity, definitions, and editorial weaknesses.

#### Advantages

- Reduces duplication.
- Allows broad review with less total output.
- Gives each system a clearly defined responsibility.

#### Disadvantages

- Review quality becomes dependent upon which system receives each category.
- Issues detected through overlap between categories may be missed.
- Results are less directly comparable.
- A weak review by one system may leave an entire category inadequately tested.

#### Recommended Use

Use this method for preliminary review, experimentation, or lower-priority
documents.

It should not ordinarily replace comprehensive parallel review for major
chapters.

## Recommended Review Strategy

For major chapters and proposals, the preferred process is:

1. Conduct a Comprehensive Parallel Review using all available AI systems.
2. Consolidate duplicate or closely related findings.
3. Identify disputed, serious, or unresolved issues.
4. Conduct Targeted Parallel Reviews on those issues.
5. Verify factual, legal, historical, and constitutional claims using
   authoritative sources.
6. Record the disposition of every material finding.
7. Revise the document.
8. Repeat the review when revisions substantially alter the argument.

Distributed Specialized Review may be used as a supplement, but should not be
the only review method for material that is approaching publication.

## Required Reviewer Instructions

Each AI reviewer should receive substantially equivalent instructions.

The reviewer should be told:

- Do not merely summarize the document.
- Do not evaluate the document according to whether you personally agree with
  it.
- Do not manufacture weak or frivolous objections.
- Identify the strongest credible objections.
- Distinguish actual errors from disagreements in philosophy or policy.
- Distinguish factual defects from interpretive disputes.
- Quote or identify the specific passage being criticized.
- Explain why the issue matters.
- Provide supporting evidence when possible.
- State uncertainty clearly.
- Do not fabricate facts, citations, quotations, cases, statutes, or sources.
- Recommend a correction or research action where practical.
- Acknowledge arguments that are strong and well-supported.
- Do not rewrite the author’s position into a different position.

## Standard Review Output

To allow findings from different reviewers to be compared, each finding should
use the following format.

### Finding Identifier

Example: `HR-MC-203-001`

### Location

Identify the chapter, section, paragraph, claim, or quoted passage.

### Category

Select one or more:

- Logic
- Assumption
- Definition
- Ambiguity
- Fact
- Missing Context
- Constitutional
- Legal
- Counterargument
- Citation
- Implementation
- Incentive
- Unintended Consequence
- Editorial
- Other

### Severity

| Severity | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Critical** | The argument or proposal cannot stand without correction. |
| **Major** | The issue materially weakens the argument. |
| **Moderate** | The issue should be addressed but does not invalidate the argument. |
| **Minor** | The issue affects clarity, precision, or completeness. |
| **Observation** | A useful consideration that may not require revision. |

### Finding

Describe the problem clearly.

### Why It Matters

Explain how the issue affects the argument, reader understanding,
constitutional analysis, factual reliability, or proposal.

### Evidence or Reasoning

Provide the basis for the criticism.

### Strongest Counterargument

State the strongest reasonable challenge to the document’s position.

### Recommended Action

Recommend one of the following:

- Correct
- Clarify
- Add evidence
- Add citation
- Add counterargument
- Narrow claim
- Revise proposal
- Conduct further research
- No change recommended

### Reviewer Confidence

- High
- Medium
- Low

## Finding Disposition

Every material finding should receive a documented disposition.

Permitted dispositions are:

### Accepted

The finding is valid and the document will be revised.

### Partially Accepted

The finding identifies a valid concern, but the proposed correction or scope is
only partly accepted.

### Rejected

The finding is not supported, misunderstands the argument, relies on inaccurate
information, or conflicts with stronger evidence.

A rejected finding must include a reason.

### Needs Research

The available evidence is insufficient to accept or reject the finding.

### Deferred

The finding is valid but belongs in another chapter, proposal, research paper,
or future revision.

### Duplicate

The finding is substantially the same as another recorded finding.

## Review Integrity Rules

A Hostile Review is defective if it:

- Criticizes a position the document does not take.
- Relies on fabricated or unverifiable information.
- Treats disagreement as proof of error.
- Ignores the document’s stated definitions.
- Demands certainty where the evidence cannot provide certainty.
- Uses slogans instead of analysis.
- Attributes motives without evidence.
- Applies different standards to favored and disfavored positions.
- Rejects a proposal solely because it is politically difficult.
- Accepts a proposal solely because it is ideologically attractive.
- Confuses current constitutional doctrine with original public meaning.
- Confuses criticism of present law with a false claim about what present law is.

The reviewer is expected to be adversarial, but also fair.

## Human Review Requirement

AI-generated review findings are advisory.

They do not replace human judgment, primary-source verification, legal research,
historical research, or editorial responsibility.

No factual, legal, historical, constitutional, or statistical criticism should
be accepted solely because an AI system stated it confidently.

Material findings must be independently evaluated.

## Completion Standard

A Hostile Review is complete when:

- [ ] The full chapter or proposal has been reviewed.
- [ ] Major arguments have been challenged.
- [ ] Material assumptions have been identified.
- [ ] Significant counterarguments have been considered.
- [ ] Findings have been consolidated.
- [ ] Each material finding has a disposition.
- [ ] Accepted findings have been corrected or assigned for correction.
- [ ] Claims requiring additional research have been recorded.
- [ ] The revised document remains consistent with the Project Charter,
      `AGENTS.md`, Style Guide, and Project Requirements Specification.

A successful Hostile Review does not mean that no one can disagree with the
document.

It means the document has confronted serious disagreement honestly, corrected
identifiable defects, and demonstrated that its conclusions are supported by a
defensible chain of evidence and reasoning.

## Governing Principle

> **Every important assertion should survive informed adversarial review.**

The objective is not to make the manuscript immune from criticism.

The objective is to ensure that criticism makes the manuscript stronger.
